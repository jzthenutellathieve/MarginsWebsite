Solutions

What's being built — and why it's not spreading fast enough.

Solutions journalism isn't advocacy. It's rigorous reporting on interventions that have evidence behind them — honest about what works, what doesn't, and what's missing. These are the two areas where we're doing that work directly.

The problem

Newly resettled refugee families in the US frequently live in housing with inconsistent utilities. Access to reliably clean drinking water is not guaranteed. Conventional filtration requires replacement cartridges and power. Bottled water costs $200–600+ per person annually. Boiling requires fuel. There is no standard solution deployed at the resettlement level.

What TENG-UV does

Triboelectric nanogenerator ultraviolet (TENG-UV) disinfection converts mechanical motion — pressing, walking, turning — into electrical charge that powers UV-C LEDs, which deactivate pathogens in water. No grid required. No chemical byproducts. No replacement parts. Efficacy exceeds 99.9% against E. coli in peer-reviewed testing published in ACS Nano.

Why it fits resettlement

The device is self-contained, portable, and durable. One unit, properly maintained, provides disinfection for years. At $8.57 per person annually, it costs less than a single case of bottled water. It requires no external infrastructure and no ongoing supply chain. These are exactly the constraints that define refugee resettlement contexts.

What a pilot would look like

A pilot with a resettlement agency would deploy devices with 10–20 families over six months, collect water quality and usage data, and produce a field report for wider dissemination. We are actively seeking partner organizations. The research collaboration is with Prof. Xingwei Wang at Tsinghua University, published under the UN-FIRST / UNESCO framework.

When a refugee family is approved for resettlement in the United States, the clock starts immediately. Federal funding covers 90 days of intensive support. After that, families are largely on their own in one of the most expensive rental markets in the country.

What Housing Navigation Actually Looks Like

Through volunteer work with Church World Service Orange County, I've observed the housing navigation process firsthand. Volunteers search Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, and direct landlord networks to find units within a family's budget. They contact landlords, explain the refugee resettlement context, assist with applications, and coordinate move-in logistics — including furniture assembly.

The limiting factor is rarely the family's qualifications. It's landlord reluctance, competition from other renters, and the narrow window in which housing must be secured before other resettlement milestones are affected.
